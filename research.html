PAGE III · RESEARCH THREE AREAS · ONE PROGRAMME
A programme of inquiry

Research at the
seam between fields.

I work where provable cryptography meets probabilistic machine learning — and where neither, on its own, suffices.

The research programme on this page is organised around three areas that look, on the surface, like distinct specialisms: machine learning security, applied cryptography, and systems / network defence. In practice I treat them as aspects of a single question: what does it mean to trust a system under adversarial pressure, and how do we justify that trust with something stronger than a benchmark?

Each area below contains projects at different stages — some published, some in preparation, some exploratory. I've included the negative results and detours where they were informative. The doctoral programme I'm preparing for extends this thread directly.

01
Machine Learning Security

Robustness, certifiable & otherwise.

04 PROJECTS · ACTIVE

Most production ML systems assume their inputs were drawn from the same distribution as their training data. That assumption does not survive contact with an adversary. My work in this area studies when and how that failure becomes tractable — the regimes where we can actually bound a classifier's behaviour under perturbation, and the regimes where we probably can't.

I take a sceptical view of empirical defences: many standard adversarial training approaches fall to slightly stronger attacks within months of publication. I'm more interested in defences with some form of certified bound — randomised smoothing, Lipschitz-constrained networks, interval-bound propagation — and in honest characterisation of the accuracy–robustness trade-off.

A note on methodology.

I try to publish negative results alongside positive ones — every project above carries, in its supplementary material, the experiments that didn't work. This is partly principled (the field has a reproducibility problem) and partly self-interested: those failures are where most of the actual intellectual content lives. If a defence works on MNIST and CIFAR-10 but not on a realistic deployment benchmark, the gap between them is the paper.

02
Applied Cryptography

Post-quantum, practical.

03 PROJECTS · ACTIVE

The cryptographic community knows that classical assumptions will eventually fail against a sufficiently capable quantum adversary. The open question is practical: how do organisations migrate without breaking compatibility with the billions of endpoints that don't yet speak post-quantum protocols?

I work on hybrid schemes — protocols that combine classical and lattice-based primitives so that an adversary must break both to compromise the session. This is not glamorous work, but it's the work that will actually determine whether the transition happens cleanly.

03
Systems & Network Security

Defences at the edge.

03 PROJECTS · MIXED

Academic security research has historically concentrated its attention on well-provisioned systems: enterprise networks, cloud infrastructure, modern browsers. But a growing share of the world's computing happens on devices that can't spare a megabyte for a TLS library, let alone a proper intrusion detection stack.

This area of my work looks at defences designed for the edge — constrained hardware, intermittent connectivity, deployment in environments without dedicated security staff. The question I find myself returning to: what is the minimum complexity a defence can have and still provide a useful guarantee?
