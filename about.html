About the researcher

A brief
portrait, in paragraphs.

I came to cybersecurity the way most people fall into their vocation — by accident. A badly-secured school wireless network and a copy of Applied Cryptography from a dusty library shelf began a line of inquiry that has, nearly a decade later, led me to the doorway of doctoral study. The questions have changed scale, but the instinct is the same: to understand how systems fail, and to build the ones that won't.

My undergraduate training in computer science sharpened a preference for working in the margins between fields. Cryptography alone is elegant but brittle in deployment; machine learning alone is powerful but epistemically troubled. The seam between them — where probabilistic systems meet provable guarantees — is where I have chosen to work.

Research temperament

I favour careful, formally-grounded work that doesn't mistake benchmark wins for progress. Much of what passes for "defence" in adversarial ML is, on inspection, empirical patching. I'm interested in the harder question: what can we prove about a system under an adversary of known budget? That's not always a tractable question, but it is the one worth asking.

In practice this means I spend equal hours reading proofs, reading logs, and writing code that breaks things on purpose. I keep a working notebook of failed attacks, because the ones that don't work are usually more informative than the ones that do.

Beyond the lab

I teach an informal reading group on applied cryptography for undergraduates at my institution, and I'm the current editor of a small student-run journal on systems research. Outside of work, I run middle-distance, collect out-of-print CS textbooks, and am learning to read classical Sinhala poetry — a discipline that has taught me more about technical writing than any style guide.

If there is one thread through all of this, it is a belief that precision is a form of care — that whether you are proving a bound, writing a protocol spec, or explaining a result to a first-year student, the effort to be exact is almost always repaid.

§ II · Principles

What guides the work.

i.

Proofs over patches

Empirical robustness is a starting point, not a result. Where possible, I pursue defences with certifiable guarantees, and treat benchmark performance as diagnostic rather than decisive.

ii.

Honest failure

Negative results are the scaffolding of a field. I document attacks that didn't land and defences that degraded under deployment as carefully as I document success.

iii.

Useful at the edges

Security that only functions on well-provisioned infrastructure leaves too much of the world unprotected. I care about protocols that run on constrained devices and in imperfect deployments.
